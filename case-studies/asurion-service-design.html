Summary

Leading service transformation

This program is a Chief Product Officer–led initiative with CEO visibility. I collaborate bi-weekly with President- and SVP-level stakeholders to set our service standards and vision—so we can drive transformational change with a shared bar for customer excellence. I co-created the service principles, service metrics, inner metrics, and tactics with the team, partnering closely with a product manager as my day-to-day thought partner. Supply chain, product, and customer solutions all weigh in as we build the ownership needed to carry the work forward at higher fidelity.

I do this through deep collaboration and workshop facilitation—organizing the organization around the biggest CX pain points and pushing for step-change, not incremental tweaks alone.

My role

Service design lead

I co-owned how principles and metrics landed in workable form, aligned outputs with teams who operationalize them, and kept facilitation, documentation, and iteration moving at the pace the program needed.

Systemic patterns

Authentication friction

Customers re-authenticate repeatedly; practices vary by team.

Data gaps

Customers repeat themselves because context does not travel with the case.

Inconsistent sales practices

Offers are hard to parse and sometimes misaligned with customer expectation.

Claims value issues

Replacements and upgrades that do not feel fair or coherent to the customer.

Support access problems

Partner-channel customers hit transfer walls; benefits are hard to use.

We built our first service blueprints in Figma. Creating them was time intensive enough, but maintaining existing ones, updating designs, and spinning up new blueprints made the ongoing cost unsustainable. We also evaluated a $70k/year SaaS tool purpose-built for service blueprinting, which turned out to be the wrong trade-off for our needs. Two bad options pushed us toward building something ourselves, which is covered in the next section.

Future service blueprint design sprint
April 7–9 · Future foundation and service metrics.
Leaders from supply chain, customer solutions, product, and marketing spent three days in current-state blueprints, metric alignment to service principles, and hands-on future-state design—including live sketching inside the blueprint web tool.
What landed

A shared picture of today’s experience, momentum on aspirational service metrics, and tangible future-state sketches—including “vibe-coded” explorations of what lifecycle care could feel like when silos matter less than the customer outcome.

  • “Wow, I had no idea this is what actually happens.”
  • “I didn’t realize how much our customers are impacted by our silos and lack of partner integration.”
  • “It was eye-opening to approach this from a customer excellence perspective versus an operational, cost-optimized perspective.”

Solidifying service metrics: workshop participants are in round-one review with domain leadership. Feedback is folding into the standards and instrumentation plan.

Service standards & instrumentation
From broken windows to promises you can run the business on.
Blueprinting surfaced concrete pain. The standards work names what “good” looks like for customers and operators, defines terminology, and gives teams a way to evaluate current experiences against an aspirational bar.
Post-service feedback
5-star survey: one rating, diagnostic tags, tied to standards.
The instrument captures a single overall star rating, then adapts the prompt and tag set by tier so customers can explain what happened in their words—while the org rolls signal up to the four standards (Easy, Fast, Fair, Human) for operational review.

Flow at a glance

  1. 5-star overall rating — one number that summarizes the engagement.
  2. Diagnose by tier — 1–2 stars: “What went wrong?” · 3 stars: “What felt off?” · 4 stars: “What could be better?” · 5 stars: “What stood out?” (Production copy continues to evolve with the same intent.)
