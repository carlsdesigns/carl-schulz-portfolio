AI-native service blueprint tool

A web application that stores, renders, and manages Asurion’s service blueprints—the operational maps from first customer click through expert intervention, fulfillment, and partner handoffs—with audience-specific views and an assistant that reads and writes inside the same structured model.

↓  Why we built it
Summary

Mapping the real service—and building the tool that scales it

I designed and built Asurion's AI-native service blueprint tool, a web application that replaced static Figma walls with structured, interactive maps featuring audience-specific views and integrated AI analysis. I authored five of the seven enterprise blueprints, auditing and mapping product experiences across the portfolio. The tool was a self-initiated response to CPO-level prompts for enterprise leverage, built end-to-end through AI-assisted development workflows to create a scalable system of record where complexity demanded systematic analysis.

Service design lead

Problem
Horizontal documents could not serve three audiences at once.
Blueprints started as single-density static maps. They worked for authors but broke down for executives scanning for decisions, for product teams who need system behavior and data-flow truth, and for enterprise partners who need orientation—not a wall of detail on day one.
◉

Executives

Signal was buried; investment narratives required reading every row.

◇

Product & platform

System-level detail—data flow, handoffs, accessibility gaps—was inconsistent or missing.

◎

Enterprise & partners

No progressive disclosure, glossary, or “how to read this” layer for first contact.

⎘

Pain points elsewhere

Annotations lived in Notion, disconnected from the map locations that produced them.

Product intent
One structured source of truth per journey.

Phases and steps form the spine. Swim lanes hold customer, expert, system, partner, supply chain, and data-flow content. Typed annotations, a first-class pain registry, props and media, threaded comments, and role-based access sit on top—then two audience views—Executive and Enterprise—tune how much signal you see without forking the underlying data.

Multi-layer viewer & reading modes

Everyone needs the same truth—from the CEO to the product manager.

We kept hearing the same frustration: almost no one knows how the service really works end to end. Ground truth on a customer or client experience still takes four or five teams before the story holds together. The viewer is for everyone who has to decide something, from leadership to platform and CX. You work with stackable lanes and overlays instead of a single frozen wall of detail. Executive view gives a quicker leadership read. Enterprise view keeps the fuller operational picture. Knowledge moves out of individual heads and into one structured surface, so aligned answers often start with a few keystrokes instead of another scavenger hunt.

Who shows up

We organize the blueprint around the people and partners who actually run the service.

Asurion is not a single-channel SaaS flow. Members and direct buyers, experts in call centers and 700+ stores and in customers’ homes, logistics that stages parts and devices, the data that keeps every handoff honest, and partners like carriers and repair depots all share custody of the same journey. Because that mix is specific to us, the map starts with these five roles—then we layer in how data shows up at each step.

The customer

Program members and direct consumers—the people asking for help, buying protection, or continuing a relationship with us.

The expert

Phone agents, store employees, and remote field experts who meet customers where the work actually happens.

Supply chain

Logistics that makes sure the right parts and devices are in motion before a job is promised.

The data

What flows across systems so customers, experts, and operations see the same truth—or reveal where they do not.

Third parties

UPS, repair depots, and other partners contracted deep into the experience—not a footnote lane, a real actor.

Same five voices across journeys, so executives, product, and enterprise partners learn the map once—then go deep on maturity and handoffs without relearning a new taxonomy each time.

Pain point registry
First-class objects—not sticky notes in another tool.
Each pain point carries classification, impact, effort, priority, status, and owner. The registry links bidirectionally to blueprint cells: click a pin on the map to open the record, or start from the registry to jump to the exact step and lane.
Props & media
Capture how the service actually looks and sounds.
Customer and expert props—UI screenshots, photos from the field, short clips—live in swim lane cells, inline thumbnails, and a lightbox for full resolution so workshops and reviews stay grounded in real artifacts.
Blueprint assistant
AI that operates inside the data model—not beside it.
A multi-provider LLM sidebar runs a server-side agent loop with tool access: read structure, inspect steps, create and update annotations, upsert swim lane content. Mandatory ordering keeps reads before writes; mutations refetch the UI so the map and chat never diverge. Because the model reads the same grid the team maintains, you can ask it to reason against Asurion’s published service standards—and get findings tied to steps, headline metrics, root causes, and dependencies.

Service Blueprint AI evaluation

The assistant ingested the live journey (annotations, lanes, data-flow cells) and scored it against the Asurion Experience Index—Easy, Fast, Fair, Human—with RED / AMBER / GREEN evidence, standard verdicts, and a sequenced remediation stack. Sequencing recommendation

The model ordered fixes by cascading dependency—foundational identity and routing before training-only interventions—so leadership sees a defensible investment path, not a flat backlog.

  1. Universal authentication — cuts across Easy + Human REDs at the front door.
  2. Expert system integration for claim data — unlocks accurate answers in Human and reduces Easy friction.
  3. Automated repair status notifications — high leverage on Fast with relatively contained build scope.
  4. Device catalog expansion (laptops) — removes a hard stop for an entire category.
  5. Support training + tool-usage protocols — targets high-visibility trust breaks after data is trustworthy.
  6. Value proposition in the claims flow — shores up Fair once navigation and auth stop leaking demand.

AI-native delivery, not only AI features

A vision-to-data pipeline converts whiteboard and PDF exports into structured import JSON for human review, then bulk loads through the API—compressing manual entry into a review-and-correct loop. The same discipline powers iterative builds of the app itself alongside coding assistants, with the PRD as shared context.

Organizational use
Workshops and leadership readouts run on the live artifact.
Future-state concepts from cross-functional sprints were captured directly in the tool so priorities, media, and comments stay attached to the journey—not lost when the workshop Zoom ends.
Implementation
Stack at a glance.

Next.js (App Router), TypeScript, Supabase Postgres with row-level security, Supabase Auth and Storage, Zustand, Tailwind CSS, multi-provider LLM integration (Gemini, OpenAI, Anthropic) with keys server-side only, Resend for mail, deployed on Vercel.
