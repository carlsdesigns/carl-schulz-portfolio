The Problem

Children need play, not pressure.

Most toddler learning apps are quiz tools in disguise. They flash a letter, wait for the right tap, and shake the screen when the child gets it wrong. For a three-year-old, that creates anxiety — not learning.

Meanwhile, parents sit next to their child and guess. No one tells them "she keeps swapping B and D" or "he hears every vowel but can't match them visually." The app gives a gold star. The parent gets nothing useful.

Children need an environment so engaging they learn by exploring it. Parents need real insight into what's clicking and what isn't — delivered as guidance, not raw data.

My Role

Designer, developer, parent

I designed and built this entire application as a personal project, motivated by wanting to help my three-year-old daughter learn her letters. I handled concept, UX design, front-end development, AI integration, image pipeline engineering, TTS implementation, and observational user testing with real toddlers.

What It Is

A playground for children, insight for parents

Bing Ding Learning is a browser-based educational app for toddlers aged 2–4. The child never takes a quiz with stakes. The parent never has to guess anymore.

A free-play letter canvas, seven AI-generated world backgrounds, three structured game modes — letter matching, sound-to-letter, and image-to-letter — plus drawing tools and voice-activated world creation. Everything a toddler touches adds to the world instead of testing them.

The AI progress report computes per-letter accuracy, confusion pairs, and trends locally — then sends a snapshot to Google Gemini, which writes a narrative summary as an early childhood specialist. Parents get a warm, specific note about what their child is mastering and where to focus next.

Free Play Canvas

No right answer. No wrong answer. No score.

The canvas fills up. Letters and pictures pile on top of each other. A child can press A forty times and get forty apples. They can press everything in the alphabet and end up with a chaotic, joyful landscape of illustrations. It's a creative sandbox where the alphabet is the toy.

The audio engine never queues utterances. If a child presses A while the voice is still speaking, haptics and images still fire, but the audio switches cleanly to the new letter. The system honors what the child is doing right now, not chasing a backlog.

AI World Generation

The child describes a world and it appears

Beyond seven pre-illustrated scenes, children and parents can describe their own world via voice or text. "A jungle with monkeys and vines." The app generates a custom watercolor background to match. Generated worlds are saved persistently. A jungle asked for on Tuesday is still there on Friday.

This is not a feature. It's a new kind of relationship between a toddler and a screen. The child is not consuming content. They are creating a world, and the world is listening.

AI Progress Report

Local computation tells you what happened. AI tells you what it means.

Statistics are computed locally and render instantly: per-letter accuracy, confusion pair detection (B→D, p→q), trend analysis, and response time patterns. Then Google Gemini writes a narrative summary as an early childhood education specialist.

The output reads like a note from someone who paid attention: "Sammy is making real progress. She's solid on most consonants and has mastered A, B, and S. The area to watch is D, where she's consistently choosing B instead, which is one of the most common mix-ups at this age and completely normal."

Technical Implementation

Client-side first. AI only where it genuinely adds something.

The app runs entirely in the browser. All game data, profiles, and computed statistics live on the device. No accounts. No backend database. No login wall. Nothing a grandparent couldn't figure out on a shared iPad.

Frontend
React, browser-native APIs. Static web app deployed to Vercel.
Persistence
Browser localStorage. Child's name = profile key. progress_sammy and progress_isabel are independent namespaces.
Voice
ElevenLabs API. All letter/number voices created once and cached on server. New requests only when referencing a new child's name.
Images
Multi-step pipeline: generate with magenta background → crop magenta with image library → save transparent PNG. Zero design tools touched.
Intelligence
Google Gemini API for progress narratives and quiz sequencing. Cached 1 hour. Falls back to statistical view if API fails.
Reuse over rebuild

Custom worlds, pre-created worlds, and runtime generation all flow through the same four-function API. Nothing is duplicated.

Degrade gracefully

ElevenLabs down? Falls back to native TTS + text label. Gemini fails? Full statistics still render. localStorage unavailable? Games still work.

Privacy as default

No accounts. No cloud storage. No images sent to AI. Only aggregated stats with a child's first name. A parent never has to wonder where their child's data is going.

Smart caching

ElevenLabs audio for all 26 letters and 10 numbers is generated once and reused across every child. Only personalized name audio requires a new API call.

Adaptive Learning Engine

AI guides the quiz — ordering, sequencing, praise, and redirection

The three game modes are not random flashcards. AI orchestrates the order and sequencing of every letter and number quiz, ensuring the child moves through the full character set at a pace calibrated to their performance. Characters the child struggles with resurface more frequently; mastered characters rotate back at longer intervals to reinforce retention.

Every five interactions, the AI generates personalized praise — referencing the child by name and calling out specific progress ("You're getting so good at the letter S, Mia!"). When a child selects an incorrect answer, the AI crafts gentle, unique redirection that reframes the mistake as exploration rather than failure, keeping the emotional tone warm and pressure-free.

Behind the scenes, every answer is tracked: target character, options shown, selection, correctness, timestamp, and response time. The AI analyzes this stream to detect confusion pairs (B→D, p→q), identify auditory-vs-visual gaps, and adjust the difficulty curve in real time. The child experiences a game that somehow always feels right. The parent sees the learning data it generates.

How the adaptive engine works

Spaced repetition

Characters the child gets wrong reappear sooner. Mastered characters cycle back at widening intervals — the same principle behind research-backed flashcard systems, adapted for a toddler who doesn't know they're studying.

Full-set coverage

The sequencer guarantees every letter and number appears across a session. No character gets skipped, even if the child has strong preferences. Exploration of the full set is the goal.

Personalized praise

AI generates unique encouragement messages that reference the child's name and recent successes. "Great job with the letter M, Sammy!" feels different from a generic "Good job!" — and the child notices.

Gentle redirection

Wrong answers never trigger negative feedback. Instead, AI crafts warm, varied responses: "That was close! Let's try again — I know you've got this." Every redirection is unique so it never feels mechanical.

Confusion pair detection

The engine recognizes known developmental pressure points — b/d, p/q, m/w, vowel clusters — and surfaces them in the progress report. Parents learn where to focus, not just that something went wrong.

Answer stream analysis

Response time, accuracy trends, and mode-specific performance are continuously analyzed. Slow correct answers signal uncertainty; fast incorrect answers signal guessing. Both inform the next question.

Design Principles

What shaped every decision

Zero-pressure for children

No scores visible to the child. No wrong-answer animations. No streaks to break. Every press is a win because every press adds something to the world.

Signal, invisibly gathered

The data collection is a side effect of play, not the point of play. The child never knows it's happening. The parent gets real information anyway.

Reuse and responsiveness

The image manager, the design system, the AI pipeline. Each built once and called everywhere. Custom worlds and pre-created worlds flow through the same four-function API.

Degrade gracefully, always

If any API is unreachable, the app falls back to the next-best local option. No broken states. No dead ends.

Privacy as a default

No accounts. No cloud storage. No images sent to AI. Aggregated stats only, with the child's first name. A parent should never wonder where their child's data is going.
