Summary

Asurion had evolved. Its website hadn't.

In January 2021, I joined a cross-functional team with Product Marketing to kick-start transformational change to asurion.com. Asurion had recently evolved from selling a single product through partner channels to offering multiple products, including direct-to-consumer offerings. But the website still prioritized new sales over helping the millions of existing subscribers who visited each month trying to resolve a tech problem.

We needed to make it easy to use Asurion's products while also supporting marketing's goals of selling products and telling Asurion's story through the product experience. The vision I pitched at Asurion's yearly product town hall set the direction: be the go-to source for tech problems.

I wrote the vision story, prototyped the concept, and recorded the narrative to make the strategy shareable and repeatable. I led the strategic framing, OKR alignment, design execution, and in-market testing of the Fix-It Bar from concept through A/B-tested launch.

Strategic Framing

Aligning through ambiguity

Getting started was not easy. Ownership was unclear and dependencies were everywhere. I led the group to an outcome-focused OKR framework and shifted the team from a delivery-focused culture to one aligned on vision, objectives, and clear ownership.

Objective 01

Make it easy to use Asurion's product

The primary focus. Increase the speed to start a claim, improve the path to find purchased services, and make it intuitive to use Asurion for any tech problem.

Objective 02

Sell Asurion's products

Increase Connected Home, Mobility Protection, Appliance+, and UBIF sales through the product experience on asurion.com.

Objective 03

Tell Asurion's story

Increase brand awareness and improve the conversion funnel by communicating the breadth of what Asurion offers within the first interaction.

Objective 04

Build a foundation for the future

Increase site performance, improve SEO rankings, maintain 100% uptime, and decrease time to publish. This infrastructure work would enable everything else.

Problem Definition

A million visitors, fragmented across dozens of dead ends

After extensive mapping and documentation of the existing service design, the scale of the problem became clear. The site was built for sales, not for the people already paying for the product.

01

No single point of authentication

Customers had to navigate multiple portals, including separate instances for a single client like Verizon. There was no unified entry point.

02

Self-routing led to dead ends

The existing experience required customers to know their provider and product before getting help. Wrong turns created dead-end experiences and pushed people to the call center.

03

Digital leakage to call center

Customers who couldn't figure out how to initiate service based on their subscription would abandon digital and call instead, driving up support costs.

System mapping: 50+ partner portals across multiple entry points
Strategic framing: do no harm metrics, product gaps, product construct & current state process flow
Approach

From provider-first to problem-first

The core insight was simple: model the experience of describing a tech problem to an expert. Instead of asking "Who is your provider?" we asked "What can we fix for you?"

Phase 01

Conversational design model

We reframed the interaction as a conversation: "I have a mobile device with a cracked screen, and I have device protection. Please help!" The intelligent router changed the paradigm from provider-first to customer-problem-first, while showcasing Asurion's full breadth of services within the first interaction.

Phase 02

Extensive testing and iteration

We ran customer testing, cross-functional collaboration sessions, code prototypes, quantitative testing plans, and A/B testing configurations. Through many iterations and prioritization sessions, the Fix-It Bar earned buy-in to become the primary customer touchpoint on asurion.com.

Phase 03

Guerrilla front-end execution

The product design team worked in guerrilla fashion, producing high-quality front-end React solutions with delightful micro-interactions. Our engineering team was stretched thin with competing priorities, so we collaborated with a designer who had front-end skills to develop a solution that could scale later, keeping the project moving.

Phase 04

The pivot to CMS-powered MVP

We hit engineering roadblocks: hosting issues, security reviews, and code reviews delayed the ideal implementation by several months. Unwilling to wait, we pivoted. The asurion.com platform had a functional CMS and a flexible component library, so we built a simplified multi-page experience instead of the single-page interactive component.

The principle was clear: test and learn quickly, let the data inform the ideal experience later.

Solution

The Fix-It Bar

Simple, human, and friendly. Tech help for important personal technology is only a few clicks away, minutes from your front door.

A new paradigm for Asurion's front door

The Fix-It Bar successfully represented Asurion's product and brand experience principles. It made booking available from the very first moment a customer lands on asurion.com, reducing the steps from problem to resolution.

The component was built to be deployed anywhere on asurion.com. It integrates with O360, and its content is configurable, allowing teams to rapidly adapt it for different use cases: device repairs, home installs, appliance service, and beyond.

Still live today. The Fix-It Bar and its underlying "problem-first" model remain a core part of the asurion.com experience. Visit asurion.com →

Reflection

This project is a case study in leading transformational change inside a new team with an ambiguous problem space. I joined without a clear mandate, built alignment across competing stakeholders, and helped shape a direction where none existed. What I brought was the ability to hold two things simultaneously: a clear long-term vision and the pragmatism to ship incrementally and learn.

The Fix-It Bar required understanding systemic complexity—50+ fragmented portals, unclear ownership, engineering constraints, cultural resistance—and finding the thread that could move people toward a shared outcome. I used human-centered research to ground the strategy in what customers actually needed, and lean practices to ship evidence rather than wait for permission. That combination—vision, human understanding, and disciplined execution—is the pattern I bring to every engagement.

Challenges & Pivots

Navigating real-world constraints

This project tested our ability to ship meaningful work through organizational complexity. Every constraint became an opportunity to learn faster.

Engineering capacity blocked ideal implementation

The team that would implement the React-based Fix-It Bar was stretched thin with competing priorities, delaying the ideal version by months.

Pivot: CMS-powered MVP

Hosting, security, and code review delays

Even when we built the front-end solution ourselves, infrastructure requirements created additional delays before deployment.

Pivot: Simplified multi-page flow

Unclear ownership across teams

As the effort kicked off, it quickly became apparent that areas of ownership had many dependencies. Alignment required a new framework.

Pivot: Outcome-focused OKRs

Culture of delivery over outcomes

The existing team culture was focused on shipping features rather than measuring impact. We needed to shift the mindset before we could move effectively.

Pivot: Vision alignment + shared metrics
